When I was a young boy, I got immersed in Folkdance. My parent both taught it, and I got dragged along.
At the time, I thought this to be a normal hobby.
When I got older, my peers thought this a hobby to be made fun of.
Now, at 35, I'm teaching dance myself, and think this is a hobby to be proud of.
I get the chance to do what I love, and I'm following in my parents footsteps.

The last 10 years or so, I've been teaching more and more, but it was only recently that I've started noticing the similarities between dancers and developers.
Now, some of this might be common sense, but me being a bit socially challenged or awkward don't always think so.

To get started, I may need to explain the difference between Folkdance and other types to see how this is relevant for this talk.
Folkdance as a whole is a group event. You don't dance solo as a rule, nor even as a couple.
You have traditional dances such as Tango or Jive, the ballroom dances if you will, but even these you don't do as a group.
I'm talking about dances where you need 6 to 16 people to perform correctly together to get through 1 dance.

People come in different personalities, and when you add a group dynamic to this, it can be hard to balance the lot of it.
Let's have a look at some dancers and see what we can glean from it.

## Dancer number 1 - The strong silent type
They started dancing later than most and aren’t an extravert. That said, in the right group they aren’t especially quiet either. They tend to observe more than they speak, but they’re present. Sadly, they're easily overlooked as well, because there's always someone better or worse to pay attention to.
They arrive at rehearsals on time and are on the floor whenever a dance is called, whether it’s one they enjoy or one they don’t. You don’t have to check if they’re ready or persuade them to participate - they simply do what’s needed.
They don’t enjoy being singled out for praise, particularly in front of the group. They’re aware of their own weaknesses, even if they’re sometimes slow to change established habits. When change does happen, it’s usually gradual rather than immediate.

This is the type of person you like in your team, but you might not get a lot of momentum going. They are dependable, and will tackle most jobs without complaint.
Changes in workflow however might be a stumbling block and derail them for a bit.
Hard work will prevail though, and they will get there eventually. A team of these are predictable, and usually devoid of conflict.

## Dancer number 2 - the extravert
Outgoing and somewhat loud, it's no big surprise that they don't really connect to Number 1 on a personal level.
This doesn't cause issues on the dancefloor, but going on an activity, if you pair them explicitely, this might not be comfortable for either for longer durations.
They are vocal about what they think, and might be a bit brash or unfiltered. They have an opinion on almost everything and are prepared to go toe to toe with you.
If you display your expertise, they're quite willing to believe you, though they might jest or joke when they catch you on mistakes.
Their quality of performing can vary wildly, and will depend on if they like the task at hand or not, and their years of experience.

You need these in a team. They won't hesitate to put their fingers on sore spots. While they don't necessarily change the momentum of your team in the short term, if you listen to them you might fix issues before they become too big to hinder you.


## Dancer number 3 - The insightful one

with Dancer number 3 we don't focus on their extra or introvertedness. is their technical aspect that we're interested in. Some dancers just see how a dance fits together. They don't need all the explanation, they use their insight and past experiences to almost divine the next steps in the dance.
They're quick to learn a new one, and can usually remember how a dance goes for months after they last did them.
While they don't necessarily do the individual steps correctly, be it due to attention or physical issues, they always get where they need to be, when they need to be there.

In a team, they get the bigger picture. They might not remember by heart how to do a full outer join, but they can explain what data they need, or you need, before you get there. They have a strong analytical mind, and are best placed early in the process of a feature.

## Dancer number 4 - the natural
The natural dancer, a dancer that can execute their steps and their movements, while talking about the weather, and helping others move through the dance with a guiding hand. They are a joy to behold, as long as they can keep from becoming frustrated by the lower quality of their peers.

As a Developer, these are the ones that usually approach the fabled 10x developer. Being a good developer does not necessarily translate to being a good educator or coach though.

A team of these are a force to be reckoned with. If they are not held back, they achieve deliveries faster (and sometimes better) than any other team.
Usually though, you'll only find one or two in a team. Then, you need them to guide inexperienced developers to achieve the greatness they are capable of.
If coaching is not their cup of tea, since not everyone is a teacher, then they are often found tackling the hardest problems they can find.
They know the codebase like the back of their hand, and remember when small bugs caused big issues.


## The musicians
I'm lucky to be able to work with live musicians, but that is an exercise of it's own. You need good communication, and good preparation, so that rehearsals and performances go smoothly.
The same is true for the non-developers in a team. Project managers and scrum masters, analysts and designers, they all have a place in the team, but they are often found to be annoying by a developer. 
The developer does not care about a pixel here or there, they would prefer a timesheet to fill itself, and being agile is more of a hindrance than just staying in the flow. But good communication with all makes the work flow so much better.
